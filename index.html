About Me

I'm into investing, skiing, start-ups, the California coastal lifestyle, and, most of all, being a dad. I've built cutting edge products, closed tens of millions in revenue from technology consulting services, built teams, championed industry-changing digital transformation projects, and achieved a couple of patents along the way.

Check out the CSNT program

Since graduating, I've been working at Hawaii Medical Service Association (HMSA) in Honolulu, Hawaii, as a Project Administrator. In this role, I've managed multiple projects and initiatives related to infrastructure technologies. Included in these initiatives is a project involving the transition of the entire organization (about 1800 staff and contractors) from Windows 7 to Windows 10. I managed the scheduling and planning, knowledge production and distribution, and communications related to incidents or general inquiries. For this project, I leveraged my knowledge of PowerShell to write a script that pulls active users from AD, parses through them, and creates deployment groups of less than 50, grouped by manager.

After reading above, it might be curious to some as to why I've made the switch from an infrastructure-based, project role to a more technical, application developer one. A few factors drive my decision:

  • A desire to move out of Hawaii
  • A burning interest in coding and development
  • A curiousity to challenge myself with something new.
  • The writing on the wall...

What I mean by "writing on the wall" is twofold.

The first aspect of it is my internal feelings about my skills and aptitudes. When I was younger, first growing in my knowledge and experience of computers and technology, I began to get this natural sensation of a knack for what I was doing. Wether it had been helping my parents fix some issue with their computers to troubleshooting my own machines by apparent intuition. It just came naturally, and I get the same feeling about coding. I simply get what's going on in the computer and the programs I work with. Why not dig deeper and start writing them?

The second aspect is likely more obvious to everyone: tech is growing. It's growing in this country, it's growing around the world, and there's a growing need for people who want to code the world into the future. Aside from my personal interests, I have a bit of altruism in me that wants to put my talent's and affinities to good use. I have ideas, I have drive, and I want to make everyone's lives easier. That way we can get back to what we enjoy doing, which isn't figuring out why our application isn't working right.
